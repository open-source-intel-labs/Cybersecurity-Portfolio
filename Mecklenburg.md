# OSINT Case Study: Employment Scam Domain Investigation (2026)

## Executive Summary
This investigation examines an unsolicited job‑offer email claiming to represent Mecklenburg Investment Inc. The message invited the recipient to interview for a “Help Desk Support Specialist” role via Microsoft Teams. Multiple inconsistencies in the email, website, and domain registration prompted a structured OSINT review. The findings confirm that the entity is part of an active employment scam designed to collect personal information and solicit payments.

## Initial Contact Review
The investigation began with an email offering an interview for a Help Desk Support Specialist position. The role itself aligned with the recipient’s technical background, and nothing about the job title was inherently suspicious. However, the email contained several subtle indicators that warranted closer examination.

The greeting was generic (“Dear Candidate”) rather than personalized, and the message used broad, non‑specific language such as being “impressed by your experience” without referencing any actual skills or qualifications. The tone resembled a mass‑distributed template rather than a targeted recruitment message. These inconsistencies did not confirm fraud on their own, but they were unusual enough to justify performing due diligence before responding.

## Website Assessment
The website at **mecklenburginvestmentcompany.com** presented significant inconsistencies. It described the company alternately as an investment advisory firm, a leasing and project management provider, a building restoration entity, and an investment banking service. These unrelated business identities suggested fabrication rather than a legitimate organization.

The site relied heavily on stock images, and several interface elements appeared non‑functional. There were no staff profiles, leadership information, or social media links. Despite claiming a history dating back to 1925, the site contained no historical documentation, press releases, or verifiable corporate footprint.

## Domain and WHOIS Findings
A WHOIS lookup revealed that the domain was created on February 15, 2026, only weeks before the email was sent. The registrant was located in Lithuania, while the physical address was supposed to be in North Carolina. No organization name appeared in the registrant details. These findings contradicted the website’s claim of a century‑long corporate history.

## Address Verification
The website listed **229 S Brevard St, Charlotte, NC 28202** as its address. Google Maps confirmed that this is a real building known as the Historic MIC Building (Mecklenburg Investment Company). However, there was no evidence linking the building’s legitimate history to the website or the job offer. The use of a real building name appears to be an attempt to create false legitimacy.

## Scam Database Confirmation
A search for the company name surfaced a matching entry in the BBB Scam Tracker. The report described a job offer delivered through Microsoft Teams, requests for personal information including a W‑4, and eventual pressure to provide credit card details for “equipment.” The report listed the same website and company name, confirming that the entity is associated with an active employment scam.

## Fraud Pattern Summary
The investigation revealed a consistent scam pattern: unsolicited outreach, a fabricated corporate website, a recently created domain, a real building name used as camouflage, and attempts to collect sensitive information. These elements align with known employment scam tactics involving identity theft and financial fraud.

## Conclusion
The entity operating under Mecklenburg Investment Inc. and the domain **mecklenburginvestmentcompany.com** is fraudulent. The job offer is part of an ongoing employment scam campaign. This investigation demonstrates the effectiveness of OSINT techniques—including domain analysis, website evaluation, identity verification, and cross‑referencing with scam databases—in identifying fraudulent actors and preventing potential harm.
